Read Case Study

TechDoc is a popular local repair company that tasked me with creating an eye catching landing page for them.

Design Process

Project Objective

They hoped a new website could help alleviate the number of phone calls they received throughout the day, as well as help advertise the business and the types of repairs that they do. Despite their popularity, they had no real online presence outside of their Facebook page.


The idea was to create a page that:


  • highlights the type of repairs that the company does.
  • provides an avenue for customers to speak to the team via something other than the telephone.
  • make them aware of the Ticketmaster service.
  • display a map and store details.
  • highlights the company’s Facebook page where they post their deals, promos, and competitions.

With these objectives in mind, I set off on designing and developing the site.

Wireframing

After figuring out the content, the blurbs, and the different sections needed for the page, I sketched out a very rough wireframe to get a feel for the layout. This was really just for myself and not for the client to see. It’s a very quick way for me to get a high level view of what the finished product could possibly look like without spending a long time poring (yep, I thought it was spelt like ‘pouring’ too) over the details.



I then tidied up the initial sketch to help better organise my thoughts and ideas and once I was happy with the general layout, the next step was to go into more details for each of the different sections. I sketched out the various layout designs and got the client’s opinions until we were happy with a final layout.



Hero Design

The hero had to be eye catching, informative, and had to draw the client in with a good CTA. With the help of Photoshop, I designed an anthropomorphised (bless you) cracked iPhone. I can barely stand it seeing a small scratch on my phone, so I hoped that it would tug on the user’s feelings and get them to request a repair. The clients quite liked the image too.


Google Map

Implementing the map was very easy with the use of Google Map’s API and documentation.

Responsive Design

The site had to look aesthetically pleasing on all screen sizes. Some developers still create separate mobile sites, but instead of this, I used media queries to rework the layout based on what looked good given the screen real estate available. I forewent the traditional method of using device specific breakpoints and used the content as my guide for implementing breakpoints. I trusted the design and I feel it lent itself well on mobile and tablet without needing to reshuffle the content too much.

Final Thoughts

I completed this site in early March and since then, I have worked on other projects as well as completed my own portfolio website. I believe my taste has matured and my eye for good design is sharper than ever. Looking at the TechDoc site now after a long break, I can see quite a few things I’d change if I were to redesign it today.


But this was my very first full web project ever. It taught me that design was a process, introduced me to the tools that I use today, and more importantly, it helped me realise that a career in web is what I really want.
